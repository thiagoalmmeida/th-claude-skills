# AI Maturity — What level are you at?

A framework to understand where you stand with AI, what that means in practice, and what your next real step of evolution looks like.

Works for any professional — technical or non-technical, any field.

---

## How to use

Always start with the guided intake flow before delivering any diagnosis. Never skip straight to the diagnosis — even if the person described their situation in detail. The intake ensures you have the minimum information needed.

---

## Guided Intake Flow

When the conversation starts, run this sequence. Ask one question at a time. Wait for the answer before asking the next.

**Step 1 — Field**

Start with:

> "Vamos descobrir em qual nível você está com IA. Primeira pergunta: qual é a sua área de atuação?
>
> 1. Design
> 2. RH / People
> 3. Marketing
> 4. Vendas
> 5. Operações
> 6. Financeiro / Jurídico
> 7. Engenharia / Tecnologia
> 8. Outra
>
> Responda com o número."

If the conversation is in English, translate the options accordingly.

**Step 2 — Current usage**

> "Como você usa IA hoje no trabalho?
>
> 1. Uso às vezes para perguntas pontuais, mas não mudei meu jeito de trabalhar
> 2. Uso com frequência para escrever, pesquisar ou sintetizar — já economiza tempo
> 3. Já automatizei alguma tarefa ou construí algo funcional com IA
> 4. Tenho sistemas ou agentes que outras pessoas também usam
>
> Responda com o número."

**Step 3 — Built something**

> "Você já construiu algo com IA que outra pessoa usa? Pode ser um agente, automação, app ou ferramenta, mesmo que simples.
>
> 1. Não, ainda não construí nada
> 2. Construí algo, mas só eu uso
> 3. Sim, outras pessoas usam o que eu criei
>
> Responda com o número."

**Step 4 — Context (optional, only if needed)**

If the answers from steps 1-3 are sufficient to determine the level, skip this step. If there is ambiguity, ask:

> "Me conta em uma frase o que você faz no dia a dia. Pode ser curto."

---

## Triangulating the Level

Use the answers to determine the level:

| Step 2 answer | Step 3 answer | Level |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1 | L0 |
| 2 | 1 | L1 |
| 2 or 3 | 2 | L2 |
| 3 or 4 | 3 | L3 |
| 4 | 3 | L3 |

When in doubt, anchor on concrete evidence. L2 requires something built. L3 requires something others use.

---

## Delivering the Diagnosis

After the intake, deliver the diagnosis in the language the person used. Always use the standard format.

---

## The 4 Levels

### L0 — Curious Passive
**What it means:** you use AI occasionally, but haven't changed any workflow for real.

**Productivity multiplier: 0x**
Not because AI doesn't work — because it hasn't truly entered your work yet.

**Signs that confirm this level:**
- Opens ChatGPT or Claude for quick questions, then closes it and goes back to the old way
- Has experimented, found it interesting, but doesn't know how to fit it into the daily routine
- Uses AI for isolated tasks that don't connect with the main work flow
- Feels AI is useful for others but isn't sure what it does for their specific work

**Examples by area:**
- HR: asked AI to write a job post once, but the selection process is still the same
- Marketing: used it to generate post ideas, but content production hasn't changed
- Design: asked for copy variations, but the Figma workflow is the same as always
- Sales: tested it to write an email, but prospecting is still manual

**What you're missing:**
Time on repetitive tasks that AI solves in seconds. Every week you do things manually that could be accelerated — reports, summaries, first drafts, synthesis. You're using less than 10% of the potential.

**Next move:**
Pick one task you do every week and redo it from scratch with AI. Not as support — as the main flow. The goal isn't perfection, it's discovering where AI truly adds value to your specific work.

---

### L1 — Competent User
**What it means:** AI is already part of your daily routine. You know how to write decent prompts, have tools you use regularly, and feel a speed difference. But you're still using AI to do the same things as before, just faster.

**Productivity multiplier: 1x**
Real speed gain, but no structural change in what you can accomplish.

**Signs that confirm this level:**
- Has a set of tools used regularly (ChatGPT, Claude, Notion AI, Copilot, etc.)
- Writes prompts with context — not generic questions, gives background and asks for specific format
- Already saves time on tasks like writing, synthesis, and research
- Hasn't built anything yet that another person uses

**Examples by area:**
- HR: uses AI to draft communications, synthesize evaluation feedback, create interview scripts
- Marketing: generates first drafts of content, researches competitors, adapts tone by channel
- Design: synthesizes user research, generates copy variations, documents design decisions
- Finance: uses it to summarize reports, draft analyses, format presentations

**What you're missing:**
You're using AI to accelerate what you already did. The next leap isn't about speed — it's about capability. Doing things you previously couldn't do alone due to lack of time, technical skill, or resources.

**Next move:**
Identify a recurring pain in your work that you never solve because "there's no time" or "I'd need someone technical." Try solving it with AI. You'll discover that many of those barriers no longer exist.

---

### L2 — Builder
**What it means:** you crossed the line. You don't just use AI — you've built with AI. An app, an agent, a system, an automation. Something that exists in the world beyond your documents and presentations.

**Productivity multiplier: 5x**
Because you don't just work faster — you do things that previously required someone else.

**Signs that confirm this level:**
- Has built something functional with AI that solves a real problem
- Has at least one automation running that saves time recurrently
- Doesn't need an engineer or developer to validate whether an idea works
- Uses AI to frame and solve problems, not just execute tasks

**Examples by area:**
- HR: created an agent that does initial resume screening with company-specific criteria; automated post-interview feedback
- Marketing: built a system that generates weekly performance reports with analysis and suggestions; created automated content adaptation flow by channel
- Design: published a functional app via vibe coding; created an agent that synthesizes research and delivers problem hypotheses
- Sales: built a dashboard with automatic call analysis; created personalized follow-up sequences by customer profile

**What you're missing:**
What you've built still mainly serves you. The next level isn't about building more things — it's about building things that elevate other people.

**Next move:**
Take an automation, agent, or system you created for yourself and make it accessible to others. Document it. Share it. Teach it. This isn't optional for those who want to be a reference — it's what separates builder from multiplier.

---

### L3 — Multiplier
**What it means:** you build infrastructure that elevates other people. Your systems, frameworks, and tools have reach beyond you. Other professionals use what you've created.

**Productivity multiplier: 10x**
Because your work compounds: each thing you publish raises the floor for those who come after.

**Signs that confirm this level:**
- Has public or internal artifacts that other people actively use
- Is consulted by colleagues on how to use AI at work
- Can teach what they do to another person in under an hour
- What they create has a life of its own — works without them present

**Examples by area:**
- HR: created a screening system the whole team uses; developed an AI evaluation framework that became the company's standard process
- Marketing: built an agent library the team uses for content production; created an AI playbook for the team
- Design: published a repository with agents and frameworks other designers use; created a public skill any professional can install
- Operations: built automation infrastructure other teams replicate; created an AI implementation guide that became an internal reference

**What you're missing:**
L3 isn't a destination — it's a practice. The risk is stopping building because you already have recognition. Maintaining this level requires continuing to publish, teach, and iterate.

**Next move:**
Choose a system or framework you use and transform it into something teachable for other people in your field. L3 sustains itself when others can replicate what you do.

---

## Diagnostic Questions

Not all questions need to be asked. Read the answers and triangulate the level.

**What has been built:**
1. Have you built something with AI that another person uses? What was it?
2. Do you have any task you've automated — something you did manually that now runs with AI?
3. Have you ever created an app, agent, automation, or tool, even a simple one?

**Day to day:**
4. At what point in your work does AI enter today? Can you describe the flow?
5. Is there any part of your work where AI hasn't entered yet and you feel it should?

**Impact on others:**
6. Have you taught anyone to use AI the way you use it? Could you do it in under an hour?
7. Do you have anything published or shared — repository, guide, process — that others find and use?

After collecting the answers, always deliver in this format:

```
WHAT YOU'RE MISSING
[What the current level doesn't deliver — specific to the person's field]

[One concrete and specific action — not generic]

HORIZON
[What L[X+1] opens for this person's career specifically]

```

## Principles

**Diagnosis without concrete evidence is worthless.**
Always anchor in something real the person did or didn't do. Avoid generalizations.

**The level is not an identity.**
No one is stuck at L0. The level describes where the person is now, not who they are.

**The gap between L1 and L2 is the most critical.**
It's where most people get stuck. The shift isn't about tools — it's about the decision to build something that didn't exist before.

**Building for others is what separates L2 from L3.**
It's not about technical complexity. It's about reach. A simple agent that 50 people use is worth more than a complex system only you use.

**Adapt examples to the person's field.**
The examples by area exist to make the diagnosis recognizable. Use the language of the person's work, not tech jargon.
